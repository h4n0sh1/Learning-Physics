Key Insight: The ionization of Zn requires energy input (IE₁ + IE₂ = 2640 kJ/mol), but the overall cell reaction releases 289 kJ/mol. How is this possible? The energy is supplied by: (1) the highly exothermic hydration of Zn²⁺ ions, (2) the precipitation of Zn(OH)₂, and (3) the reduction of MnO₂ at the cathode. The coupled reactions make the overall process spontaneous.
Atomic-Level Ionization Mechanism
1
Surface Zn Atom
Zn atom at metal surface. 4s² electrons have highest energy, weakest binding to nucleus.
2
OH⁻ Adsorption
Hydroxide ions adsorb onto Zn surface, creating local negative charge density.
3
Electron Transfer
External circuit provides lower-energy path. Electrons tunnel to wire, driven by potential difference.
4
Zn²⁺ Formation
Loss of 4s² electrons leaves Zn²⁺ with stable [Ar]3d¹⁰ configuration.
5
Product Stabilization
Zn²⁺ immediately coordinates with OH⁻ to form Zn(OH)₂. Precipitation drives equilibrium forward.
Step 1 Details: In metallic zinc, 4s electrons are delocalized across the metal lattice (metallic bonding). Surface atoms have dangling bonds and reduced coordination, making their valence electrons more accessible for oxidation. The work function of Zn is 4.3 eV.
