Those released electrons accumulate on the electrode, creating high "electron pressure." They desperately want to leave but can't go through the electrolyte - they must go through the external wire.

Manganese dioxide at the cathode accepts electrons that arrive through the external wire. It combines with water and electrons to form MnOOH and regenerates hydroxide ions.

This electrode has a deficit of electrons - it's chemically "hungry" for them. This creates low "electron pressure" compared to the anode.

The difference in pressure between anode (high) and cathode (low) is what we call voltage - about 1.5V for this chemistry.

üü° Electrolyte - The Ion Highway

The electrolyte (KOH dissolved in water) serves a critical purpose: it conducts ions but NOT electrons.

This is what forces electrons to go the "long way" through the external circuit. If electrons could go directly through the electrolyte, the battery would short-circuit internally and produce heat instead of useful work.

üü¢ External Circuit - Where Work Happens

The wire provides a path for the free electrons in copper to drift from the high-pressure anode to the low-pressure cathode.

The light bulb (load) has a thin filament - a resistor. Electrons lose energy pushing through it. That energy becomes heat ‚Üí light.

Remember: individual electrons move slowly (~0.1 mm/s). But the "push" propagates instantly because the wire is already full of electrons. Push one in, one comes out the other end immediately.

üí° The Key Insight: Conservation

Charge is conserved: Every electron that leaves the anode arrives at the cathode. Nothing is created or destroyed - charge just moves in a circle.

Energy is conserved: Chemical bond energy (stored in zinc) is converted to electrical energy, then to heat/light. The total is constant - we're just transforming it from one form to another.

Mass is conserved: Zinc atoms become zinc ions in solution. MnO‚ÇÇ becomes MnOOH. Atoms rearrange but don't disappear. When the zinc runs out, the battery is dead.
